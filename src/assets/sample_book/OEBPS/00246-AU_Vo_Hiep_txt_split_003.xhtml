Readers’ Thoughts
on The Road to Freedom

 

(These are translations of some ideas about the Vietnamese version of this story.)

 

You were born during an era of war. World War II had just ended; the war between Communist and Nationalist in China had started; it was followed by the Korean War and the Vietnamese-French war. Because of that, you suffered a great deal of misery from a very young age. I myself worked for the French for more than ten years, but I secretly participated in the Viet Minh Front because of my patriotism until 1953. I withdrew after witnessing a cruel public trial at which they killed a landlord.

During nine years of fighting against French domination and a couple of decades of living under the suspicion of the South Vietnamese government, I could not earn enough money to support our family, so you had to struggle to earn your own living. Although you were only seven or eight years old, you had to work hard at the farm at Tan Phuc, a remote rural village in the countryside. Perhaps this was a mistake of mine, participating in the Viet Minh Front? However, the hardship and the lack of food and clothes during the war against the French helped you to gain a strong will, courage, and to overcome obstacles and danger in life.

After reading your true story, The Road to Freedom, I know that many times you were confronted with powerful, bad authorities and fearlessly faced danger to save or help weak and poor people. You have acted chivalrously; you can be proud, and we also can be proud of you. This story lets everyone know about a black period of Vietnam’s history.

—Toan Vo, Author’s Father
San Jose, California, July 2, 2001

I know you as an unyieldingly upright, self-reliant person since a young age. However, after reading your true story, The Road to Freedom, I recognize that you have done many things I never even knew about. Because of your kindness and helpfulness to those in danger, you helped them and did not care for your own safety. Such was the case when you rushed into a burning house to save the possessions of the owner. You bravely withstood people who abused their power; you fearlessly confronted violence.

I am very proud of your actions, but hard must be sometimes soft. Many times, your unsubmissive, stubborn attitude may have endangered your life. Many parts of the story scare me to death.

Nevertheless, today, it is all over. Besides working many hours at a factory, you use your spare time thinking of the past; you wrote this story in simple, easily understood sentences, and sometimes, you compose touching poems. You have a very good memory.

—Viet Lien, Author’s Mother
San Jose, California, July 1, 2001

It is a privilege to have read your work. You have written about a very emotional and tumultuous part of your life, and I am very honored to have helped you with this manuscript.

You were a very courageous child, and writing about your experiences during the war is very brave and admirable. You have opened a window for your readers to see how life was during that time and what sacrifices had to be done to survive that period.

Your manuscript is powerfully written, making sure that not only does it reflect your life, but good research was also done to keep the facts straight with the narrative. I am very proud to have worked on such a book with you.

—Hope Edwards
Developmental editor
